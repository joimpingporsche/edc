Overview

The following modeling guidelines apply to all fact sheets that can be created by a user with the

standard member role. These modelling guidelines must be observed and followed in any case.

The implementation of the guidelines is tracked and tracked as part of the regular quality

assurance measures.

If you have any questions about the guidelines described or if you need support, LeanIX support

will be happy to help!

=== PAGE 2 ===
General Modeling Rules

📘Subscriptions

In LeanIX, subscriptions are used to assign individuals and roles to a Fact Sheet. There are two

main types of subscriptions:

• Responsible: Individuals accountable for the content and maintenance of the Fact Sheet.

• Observer: Individuals who are informed but not responsible for the content.

The specific roles that can be assigned to a Fact Sheet are defined in the modeling guidelines for

each Fact Sheet type.

🔹 Responsibility Requirement

Each Fact Sheet must have at least one person assigned as Responsible. This person (or

persons):

• Is accountable for the accuracy, completeness, and currency of the Fact Sheet.

• Receives notifications about changes to the Fact Sheet.

• Acts as the primary contact for questions, clarifications, or requirements related to the

Fact Sheet.

🔹 Change of Responsibility

If a Responsible person:

• Changes roles,

• Leaves the company or department,

• Or is no longer involved with the Fact Sheet,

…it is their duty to ensure a successor is assigned to maintain the Fact Sheet.

🔹 Automated Quality Checks

LeanIX includes automated processes to ensure data quality. These processes include:

1. Inactive Responsible Person

If a Responsible person is no longer active in the company or subsidiary (e.g., due to

departure), the system checks for other Responsible persons:

o If none exist, the direct manager of the former Responsible is identified and

automatically assigned as the new Responsible.

=== PAGE 3 ===
o The manager also receives a To-Do to assign a new, appropriate Responsible

person.

o This escalation continues up to the department head level if necessary.

2. No Responsible Assigned

If no Responsible is assigned to a Fact Sheet:

o The system uses the "Responsible IT Department" relation to identify the

appropriate manager.

o This manager is then assigned as the Responsible person and is expected

to nominate a qualified business owner.

📘 Lifecycle Management in LeanIX Fact Sheets

Each Fact Sheet type in LeanIX can be assigned a lifecycle. Whether this assignment

is mandatory depends on the specific Fact Sheet type and is indicated on its detail page.

🔹 Recommended Practice

Regardless of whether the lifecycle is mandatory, it is strongly recommended to provide the

following information:

• Start of productive use (Go-Live date)

• Decommissioning date (if known)

Depending on the desired level of detail, additional lifecycle milestones can also be modeled

(e.g., development start, pilot phase, sunset planning).

🔹 Benefits

Accurate lifecycle data supports:

• Strategic planning and portfolio management

• Compliance with regulatory and audit requirements

• Impact analysis for changes and decommissioning

• Architecture governance across all layers

📘Organizational Responsibilities

Each Fact Sheet in LeanIX must include an assignment of organizational responsibility. This

responsibility can be defined at two levels:

=== PAGE 4 ===
• Organizational Structure Level (e.g., departments, business units)

• Process Organization Level (e.g., Agile Release Trains, portfolios)

For certain Fact Sheet types, a distinction may also be made between functional (business)

responsibility and technical (IT) responsibility.

✅ At least one form of responsibility must be specified—either at the

organizational structure level or the process organization level.

🔄 Ideally, both levels should be documented to ensure full traceability and

accountability.

⚠ If IT responsibility is defined as mandatory in the modeling guidelines for

a specific Fact Sheet type, it must be provided without exception.

Responsibility at the Organizational Structure Level

At Porsche, the organizational structure is defined by the official organizational chart, which

organizes the company into hierarchical levels such as:

• Division

• Main Department

• Department

• Subdepartment (lowest level)

In LeanIX modeling, unless explicitly stated otherwise, responsibility must

always be assigned to the lowest available level of the organizational structure.

🔹 Example

If an IT Product is operationally managed by the FDB1 department, then the organizational

Fact Sheet for FDB1 must be assigned in LeanIX as the responsible unit.

❌ Assigning the higher-level unit FDB would be incorrect and violate modeling

standards.

🔹 Key Principles

• Always reference the most granular organizational unit available.

• Ensure that the assigned unit reflects the actual operational responsibility.

• This ensures clear accountability, accurate reporting, and effective governance.

=== PAGE 5 ===
Responsibility at the Process Organization Level

Many IT products and applications at Porsche are developed and operated by Agile Release

Trains (ARTs). Others are managed within specific portfolios.

🔹 Assignment Guidelines

• If an ART is responsible for a product (or any other relevant Fact Sheet type), this

responsibility must be explicitly documented in LeanIX by assigning the corresponding

ART Fact Sheet.

• If the responsibility lies with a single portfolio—as is often the case with P51

applications—then assigning the portfolio is sufficient.

✅ Always ensure that the assigned ART or portfolio accurately reflects

the operational ownership of the product.

🔹 Key Principles

• Use the ART Fact Sheet when agile teams are responsible for development and

operations.

• Use the Portfolio Fact Sheet when responsibility is anchored in a business or IT

portfolio.

• This ensures clear accountability, supports agile governance, and aligns with LeanIX

modeling standards.

Please note

Only organizational fact sheets that are tagged with "Synced" or "Manual" may be used! The

use of fact sheets with the tag "Legacy" is not allowed!

📘 Modeling Version History with Successor and

Predecessor Relations

In LeanIX, the Successor and Predecessor relations are used to model the version history of

Fact Sheets over time.

These relations should be applied whenever a new version of an existing Fact Sheet is created

to replace an older version.

=== PAGE 6 ===
✅ Successor relations can also be used while the previous version is still

active and in productive use.

Best Practice for Unused Relations

If no predecessor or successor exists for a given Fact Sheet, it is recommended to explicitly set

the relation field to:

“Leave empty”

This ensures clarity in the data model and avoids ambiguity in version tracking.

🔹 Key Principles

• Use Successor/Predecessor to document version transitions.

• Apply even if the older version is still operational.

• If not applicable, set the relation field to “Leave empty” instead of leaving it undefined.

📘 Modeling Hierarchies with Parent/Child Relations in

LeanIX

In LeanIX, Parent/Child relations are used to model hierarchical dependencies between Fact Sheets. These

relations help structure complex systems, portfolios, or organizational units by defining logical

containment or ownership hierarchies.

🔹 Usage Guidelines

• A Parent Fact Sheet represents a higher-level entity that logically contains or governs

one or more Child Fact Sheets.

• A Child Fact Sheet is a subordinate entity that inherits context or structure from its

parent.

This relation is commonly used in the following scenarios:

🔹 Special Rule for IT Products

⚠ For the Fact Sheet type "IT Product", the Parent/Child relation must only

be used after prior consultation with the responsible Portfolio Architect and

the LeanIX team.

This ensures that hierarchical modeling aligns with enterprise architecture

principles and avoids misrepresentation of product structures.

🔹 Best Practices

• Use Parent/Child relations to group related Fact Sheets under a common context.

• Ensure that the hierarchy reflects actual ownership or architectural structure.

• Avoid circular or ambiguous relationships.

• Document the rationale for the hierarchy in the description field of the Fact Sheet if

needed.

• ✅ If the Parent/Child relation is not applicable, explicitly set the field to “Leave

empty” to ensure clarity and data quality.

🔹 Benefits

• Improves navigability and traceability in LeanIX.

• Supports impact analysis and portfolio planning.

• Enables structured reporting and governance alignment.
